Thesis Silicon cohort · results
Method validation · LLM-as-judge

Can an AI read a conversation and recover how it made you feel?

We built 20 “silicon participants” from real open-data personality profiles, had each one actually live the study — chatting with the same future-self chatbot real students use — then asked an independent AI judge to predict their self-reported feelings from the transcript alone. It’s a full dry-run of the thesis’ evaluation pipeline, end to end, before a single human session.

N=20 silicon participants profiles from 145,828-person open dataset participant Opus  ≠  judge Sonnet
The idea

A study that runs itself — so the method can be tested before people are.

Three moves turn a static dataset into a living agreement experiment:

01 — REAL INPUTS

Real profiles in

Each persona is a real respondent from a 145,828-person RIASEC + Big-Five open dataset — genuine trait and interest patterns, filtered to our undergraduate business/econ frame.

02 — IT LIVES THE STUDY

The persona participates

An Opus participant plays that student — picks a career, talks to the same Sonnet future-self bot the humans use, and fills in the continuity / vividness / closeness questionnaires before and after.

03 — A JUDGE GUESSES

An independent judge scores

A separate Sonnet judge reads only the transcript and predicts those ratings. Participant ≠ judge, so there’s no way to trivially agree with itself — the core anti-circularity guard.

Why that’s cool: it exercises every moving part of the real research question — persona depth, prompt structure, the judge, the metrics — on lifelike data, for the price of API calls instead of months of recruitment. If the pipeline is going to break or mislead, it shows here first.

Finding 1 · Face validity

The conversation actually moves people.

Mean shift in self-reported feelings, before → after the chat

Across all 20 silicon participants, every future-self outcome rises after the conversation — exactly the Identity-Based-Motivation direction the study predicts. The personas aren’t flat responders; they react like people who just had a vivid glimpse of their future. Scales are 1–7.

0+1+2+3+3.5
Finding 2 · The RQ metric

How well the judge agrees, by how much it’s told.

Rank agreement (Spearman ρ) — judge’s predicted rating vs the participant’s self-report

Each cell is one outcome at one persona depth (D0 = demographics only, D2 = + the person’s own words, D3 = a deliberate leakage probe). Warmer = stronger agreement. The standout is continuity at D0 (ρ = 0.64); elsewhere agreement is weak — honest for a 20-person pilot, and a clear map of where the judge already works and where it doesn’t.

ρ ≤ 0ρ → 0.85
Finding 3 · The interesting one

The judge can’t read the level — but it tracks the change.

Predicting the absolute rating vs. predicting the shift from baseline

Absolute agreement (ICC) is near zero — the judge systematically under-reads how good people say they feel. But ask a different question — did this conversation move the person, and how much? — and agreement on the change jumps to ρ ≈ 0.8 for continuity. The judge is a reliable change-detector, even when it’s a poor absolute thermometer. That reframes what this kind of evaluator is actually good for.

Finding 4 · A consistent personality

The judge is a careful skeptic.

Average gap: judge’s prediction − participant’s self-report (points, 1–7 scale)

Every bar points the same way: the judge, working only from the transcript, rates feelings lower than the participants rate themselves — most of all on the manipulation checks. It’s not random noise; it’s a stable, correctable calibration offset. Knowing the bias is consistent is half the fix.

−2.5−1.50

Why it matters

What this buys the thesis.

✓

The pipeline is proven end-to-end

Profiles → lived study → judged transcripts → agreement metrics all run on lifelike data. The machinery for the real research question is verified and waiting for human sessions.

↗

It found a real, usable result

The judge is weak on absolute levels but strong on change (ρ≈0.8). That’s a finding about what LLM evaluators are for — measuring movement, not magnitude.

🛡

It de-risks the human study

The systematic under-reading and the depth pattern are visible now, cheaply — so the instruments, rubric, and calibration can be fixed before scarce participant data is spent.

⚖

It’s honest by construction

Participant and judge are different models; depth D3 is a labelled leakage probe; every claim here is framed as a pilot. The method shows its own seams.

Read this the right way

  • Method validation, not human ground truth. Inputs are real; behaviour and ratings are AI-generated — this measures LLM↔LLM agreement (a dry-run), not LLM↔human.
  • N = 20 → descriptive only. Correlations are unstable at this size; treat every number as a pilot signal, not a result.
  • Single judge model (Sonnet), and persuasiveness isn’t collected by the app — so it isn’t shown.
