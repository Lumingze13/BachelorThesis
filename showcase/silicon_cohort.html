Silicon participants · fidelity study

Can an AI stand in for a real study participant?

My thesis turns real psychometric profiles into LLM “silicon participants,” runs each one through the entire future-self study, and measures how faithfully their responses match real people’s — and how the design of the simulation changes that fidelity. Below: the question, the apparatus (built and validated), the supporting evidence, and the one thing it still needs.

N=30 silicon participants profiles from a 145,828-person open dataset model held fixed: Sonnet
The research question

One question, four comparisons.

The question

How do design choices in an LLM-based participant simulation affect its agreement with human responses on the future-self outcomes (continuity, vividness, closeness) — for a 10-year career future-self chatbot?

Independent variable

The design of the simulation — how richly the persona is conditioned (demographics → + psychometrics → + the person’s own words), and the participant model.

Dependent variable

Fidelity to humans — how closely the simulated responses match real participants’ on the same IBM outcomes.

What “fidelity” means — four levels.

①

Means & change

Do silicon and humans land in the same place, and move the same amount pre→post?

②

The condition effect

Does the simulation reproduce the human design effect — same direction and size?

③

Variance & shape

Do simulated responses spread like real people’s, or flatten? (SD ratio, KS distance.)

④

Person-level structure

Do the same trait→outcome links hold (e.g. high neuroticism → lower closeness) in both?

Status

  • The simulation, the metrics, and this page are built and validated. The one missing input is a human benchmark to compare against — ideally per-participant. The evidence below shows the apparatus is ready and that the design choices already move the outcomes.
The apparatus

Build the participant, not just the chatbot.

A silicon participant is assembled and run end-to-end in three moves:

01 — REAL PROFILE

Start from a real person

Each persona carries the real Big-Five + RIASEC + demographics of one respondent in a 145,828-person open psychometrics dataset, filtered to an undergraduate business/economics frame.

02 — IT LIVES THE STUDY

Run the whole pipeline

An LLM plays that person — self-reports the pre-survey, talks to the future-self bot, self-reports the post-survey — producing a session in the exact shape a human session would.

03 — COMPARE TO HUMANS

Measure the fidelity

Compare the simulated outcomes to human results — means, the pre→post effect, and the variance. The human side is the next step; first we test how robust the simulation itself is.

Supporting evidence · 1 of 2

The simulated participants react like people.

Mean shift in self-reported feelings, before → after the conversation (N=30, 1–7 scale)

Every future-self outcome rises after the chat — the Identity-Based-Motivation direction the study predicts. The personas aren’t flat responders; they move like someone who just had a vivid glimpse of their future. A necessary sanity check before asking whether they match humans.

Supporting evidence · 2 of 2

The simulation’s design already moves the result.

Persona-source ablation — full psychometric profile vs. baseline questionnaire only

A first turn of the RQ’s independent variable, run within silicon (same model, same bot, same 30 people; only the persona conditioning changes). Stripping the Big-Five/RIASEC profile leaves the means statistically unchanged (paired-t p = 0.08–0.74) but significantly collapses the between-person variance on 3 of 4 outcomes — SD roughly halves. Each pair is the spread (SD) of an outcome across the 30 participants under each persona source.

Two things this establishes for the RQ: (1) a design choice measurably changes the simulated outcome — so the IV is real and consequential; and (2) the change shows up as variance — the “LLMs reproduce means but flatten variance” effect, and the dimension most likely to diverge from real people. That makes variance the comparison to watch once human data lands.

What’s next

Everything for the RQ is in place. To answer it, the simulated outcomes need real participant outcomes to be compared against — then the four fidelity levels above can be computed.

Read this the right way

  • Within-silicon, not vs. humans (yet). Both arms are AI — this measures robustness to persona construction, and previews the variance question. The headline fidelity-to-humans result needs human data (Thy/Kaehl, per-participant).
  • N = 30, single run per arm (k=1). The variance gap isn’t yet separated from run-to-run LLM noise — a small k=3 repeat would pin the noise floor. Per-participant correspondence is weak (r ≈ 0.2–0.4, CIs mostly include 0).
  • “Questionnaire-only” still keeps the baseline self-report; what’s removed is Big-Five + RIASEC. The bot used the full profile in both arms, so only the participant persona changed.
